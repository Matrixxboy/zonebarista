## CO₂ Production During Roasting

During roasting, multiple chemical reactions produce CO₂:

### Sources of CO₂

| Reaction | Temperature | CO₂ Source |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Maillard reaction | 140–200°C | Degradation of amino acids + sugars |
| Caramelisation | 160–220°C | Thermal degradation of sucrose |
| Chlorogenic acid pyrolysis | 180–220°C | Decomposition of CGAs |
| Cellulose/lignin pyrolysis | > 200°C | Cell wall decomposition |
| Lipid oxidation | Throughout | Fatty acid degradation |

### CO₂ Trapped in Bean

The roasted bean is a **porous solid matrix** — CO₂ is trapped in:
- Cell wall microstructures
- Oil-coated surfaces (physisorption)
- Dissolved in coffee oils (absorption)

**Typical CO₂ content:**
- Light roast: 4–8 mL CO₂ per gram of coffee
- Medium roast: 8–12 mL/g
- Dark roast: 10–16 mL/g (more pyrolysis → more CO₂ produced)

## Degassing Kinetics

### Release Pattern

CO₂ release follows a **bi-exponential decay** — fast initial release followed by slow long-term degassing:

$$C(t) = A_1 \cdot e^{-k_1 t} + A_2 \cdot e^{-k_2 t}$$

Where:
- *A₁*, *A₂* = initial amounts of fast and slow fractions
- *k₁*, *k₂* = rate constants (fast and slow)
- *t* = time since roasting

```
CO₂ Release Rate (relative units)

100% ─ ● ← Immediately post-roast (peak)
 80% ─   ●
 60% ─     ●●
 40% ─       ●●●
 25% ─          ●●●●
 15% ─               ●●●●●●
 10% ─                       ●●●●●●●●●●
  5% ─                                 ─────────
      0   2   4   6   8  14    21   30    45  days
      └──fast──┘└─────moderate─────┘└───slow────┘
```

### Factors Affecting Degassing Rate

| Factor | Effect on Degassing Rate |
|--------|--------------------------|
| Higher temperature (storage) | Faster degassing |
| Finer grind | Much faster (more surface area) |
| Darker roast | Higher initial CO₂; faster initial release |
| Humidity | Slight acceleration |
| Nitrogen flushing (packaging) | Slows oxidation; no effect on CO₂ rate |
| Valve bag | Allows CO₂ out; keeps O₂ out |

## Effects on Extraction

### CO₂ and Channeling (Espresso)

Freshly roasted coffee (< 5–7 days) causes channeling because:

1. High CO₂ content → CO₂ dissolved in water under 9 bar
2. As pressure drops at basket edge → CO₂ comes out of solution → bubbles form
3. Bubbles disrupt water flow → preferential pathways → channeling

**Visual sign:** Spritzy, uneven flow; large bubbles in crema; white foam

**Solution:** Rest espresso 7–14 days post-roast

---

### CO₂ and the Bloom (Filter Coffee)

The **bloom** is the visual CO₂ release during pour-over wetting:

```
Bloom mechanics:
1. Hot water contacts ground coffee surface
2. CO₂ (trapped in bean matrix) rapidly expands and escapes
3. Grounds swell visibly; "bubble" and rise
4. CO₂ physically prevents water from penetrating grounds
5. 30–45 second bloom allows CO₂ to escape
6. Now water can penetrate evenly → uniform extraction
```

**Bloom volume indicator:**
- Large, dramatic bloom → very fresh (< 1 week)
- Moderate bloom → fresh (1–3 weeks) — optimal
- Minimal bloom → 3–6 weeks — still good
- No bloom → stale (> 6 weeks unsealed) — replace

---

### CO₂ and Espresso Crema

CO₂ is essential for crema formation:

$$\text{Henry's Law: } C_{CO_2} = k_H \cdot P_{CO_2}$$

At 9 bar: CO₂ dissolves into water at much higher concentration.

When espresso exits basket at atmospheric pressure → CO₂ comes out of solution → forms fine bubbles stabilised by melanoidins and proteins → **crema**.

| Coffee Freshness | Crema |
|-----------------|-------|
| Very fresh (< 5 days) | Excessive CO₂ → white bubbles on crema; unstable |
| Fresh (7–21 days) | ✅ Optimal crema — thick, persistent, golden |
| Ageing (3–6 weeks) | Thinner crema; lighter colour |
| Stale (> 6 weeks) | Minimal crema; fast dissipation |

## Coffee Staling — Chemistry

Staling is distinct from CO₂ loss — it involves **chemical degradation** of flavour compounds:

### Primary Staling Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Compounds Affected | Flavour Impact |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Oxidation** | Volatile aromatics, lipids | Loss of brightness; rancid off-notes |
| **Hydrolysis** | Esters, some aromatics | Loss of fruity notes |
| **Loss of volatiles** | All volatile aromatics | Flat, muted aroma |
| **Lipid oxidation** | Cafestol, fatty acids | Rancid, cardboard notes |
| **Maillard product degradation** | Melanoidins | Loss of sweetness |

### Oxidation Kinetics

$$\text{Oxidation rate} \propto [O_2] \times \text{Temperature} \times \text{Surface Area}$$

**Implications:**
- Oxygen is the primary enemy of fresh coffee
- Whole beans oxidise ~100× slower than ground coffee (much less surface area)
- Refrigeration slows staling but introduces moisture risk
- Freezing pauses staling effectively if done correctly

## Freshness Windows

### Whole Bean (Sealed Valve Bag)

| Roast Level | Peak Freshness | Acceptable | Stale |
|------------|---------------|------------|-------|
| Light roast | Days 5–21 | 3–6 weeks | > 8 weeks |
| Medium roast | Days 5–28 | 4–8 weeks | > 10 weeks |
| Dark roast | Days 3–21 | 3–6 weeks | > 8 weeks |

### Whole Bean (Once Opened)

- **Reseal tightly after every use**
- Consume within 2–3 weeks of opening
- Store in opaque, airtight container at room temperature (not fridge)

### Ground Coffee

| Ground | Peak | Maximum |
|--------|------|---------|
| Immediately pre-brew | ✅ Optimal | Grind fresh always |
| Ground, airtight | 24–48 hours | 3–4 days |
| Ground, open air | 30 minutes | 1 hour |

> 🔑 **Grind fresh.** The difference between freshly ground and 24-hour-old ground coffee is dramatic. Surface area increase after grinding accelerates staling by ~100×.

## Freezing Coffee

### The Science

Freezing effectively **pauses** all staling reactions:
- Oxidation stops
- Volatile loss slows dramatically
- CO₂ degassing slows

### Correct Freezing Protocol

```
FREEZING WHOLE BEANS (recommended method)

1. Divide into single-use portions (50–200g)
2. Package in airtight, moisture-proof bags (vacuum-seal ideal)
3. Label: coffee name + roast date + portion size
4. Freeze immediately after portioning
5. Store up to 6 months

THAWING PROTOCOL (critical)
1. Remove one portion from freezer
2. Do NOT open the bag
3. Allow to reach room temperature completely (~1–2 hours)
4. Only then open and use immediately
5. Never refreeze a thawed portion
```

**Why don't open when cold?** Cold coffee immediately condenses atmospheric moisture → water on the beans → rapid oxidation and flavour loss.

## Storage Best Practices

### Recommended Storage

| Condition | Recommendation |
|---------|---------------|
| Container | Opaque, airtight; ceramic or stainless > plastic |
| Location | Cool, dark cupboard (18–22°C ideal) |
| Away from | Heat (oven, kettle), light, moisture, strong odours |
| Duration | Within 4 weeks of roast date (whole bean) |
| Refrigerator | **Not recommended** (moisture condensation on warm-to-cold cycle) |
| Freezer | Acceptable if done correctly (see above) |

### What to Avoid

| Practice | Why It's Wrong |
|---------|---------------|
| Clear glass jar on counter | Light + temperature fluctuation → rapid staling |
| Open top container | O₂ and moisture access |
| Fridge (open bag) | Absorbs fridge odours; moisture risk |
| Near stove | Heat accelerates all staling reactions |
| Buying pre-ground | Surface area → stales in hours |
| Big bulk purchase | Old stock by end of bag |

## Freshness Testing Methods

### Consumer Methods

| Test | How | Indicates |
|------|-----|-----------|
| **Bloom test** | Pour-over bloom size | CO₂ remaining → freshness |
| **Smell** | Dry grounds aroma | Fresh = intense; stale = muted/cardboard |
| **Crema check** | Espresso crema thickness/persistence | CO₂ content |

### Professional Methods

| Test | Tool | Measures |
|------|------|---------|
| **Headspace GC** | Gas chromatography | Specific volatile compounds |
| **CO₂ content** | Gas analyzer | mL CO₂ per gram |
| **Sensory cupping** | Standard SCA cupping | Overall freshness and quality |
| **Staling index** | GC-MS | 2-methylfuran ratio (staling marker) |
