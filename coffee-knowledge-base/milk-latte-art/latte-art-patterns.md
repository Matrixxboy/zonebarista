## Physics of Latte Art

### Why Patterns Form

Latte art works through **density differential and surface tension**:

1. **Espresso + crema** form a dark, viscous surface layer
2. **Microfoam** (milk + foam) has slightly lower density than espresso → floats on top
3. When poured close to the surface, microfoam sits on top of espresso → creates white pattern
4. The contrast between white foam and dark crema is the visible pattern

```
Cross-section of a latte art pour:

High pour (filling):           Low pour (art phase):
    ┌──────────────┐               ┌──────────────┐
    │  (airspace)  │               │ ←WHITE FOAM→ │
    │    ↓↓↓       │               │ ←DARK ESPRESSO│
    │  milk falls  │               │              │
    └──────────────┘               └──────────────┘
Milk sinks under crema        Foam floats on surface
```

## Prerequisites for Latte Art

| Requirement | Standard |
|------------|---------|
| Espresso crema | Intact, even, not dissipated |
| Microfoam | Silky, glossy, no visible bubbles, pourable |
| Cup angle | ~45° during pour |
| Pitcher | Comfortable grip; narrow spout preferred |
| Milk temperature | 60–65°C (cooler milk moves better) |

## Pattern 1: Heart ♥

**Difficulty:** Beginner | **Time to learn:** 1–2 weeks practice

### Step-by-Step

```
Step 1:           Step 2:           Step 3:           Step 4:
High pour         Lower pitcher     Wiggle slightly   Raise + cut
                  to surface        (optional)        through center

 ╔══╗             ╔══╗              ╔══╗              ╔══╗
 ║  ║  ←high      ║  ║              ║  ║              ║  ║
 ║↓↓║             ║●─║←low          ║●~║←wiggle       ║↑/║
 ╚══╝             ╚══╝              ╚══╝              ╚══╝
 Fill cup to 40%  Start white       Blob forms        Cut through
                  circle                              to make point
```

**Detailed technique:**
1. **High pour phase:** Hold pitcher high (15–20cm above cup); pour steadily; fill to ~40% volume. Milk sinks under crema — no pattern yet.
2. **Lower pitcher:** Bring spout close to espresso surface (1–2cm); pour into center of cup
3. **Create blob:** White foam begins appearing on surface; keep pouring until you see a white circle forming
4. **Cut through:** Raise pitcher slightly; pour a thin stream through center of white blob from front to back
5. **Lift away:** The cutting motion creates the two lobes and the point of the heart

**Common mistakes:**
- Pouring too slowly → milk cools; pattern doesn't form
- Not lowering pitcher enough → foam sinks, no pattern
- Cutting too slowly → indistinct point
- Cup not angled → milk flows to one side

## Pattern 2: Rosetta 🌿

**Difficulty:** Intermediate | **Time to learn:** 2–8 weeks practice

### Step-by-Step

```
Step 1:    Step 2:    Step 3:    Step 4:    Step 5:
Fill 40%   Lower      Wiggle     Move back  Cut through

           ↔↔↔↔        ↔↔↔        ↔↔↔
  ↓↓       ↓           ↓ ←move   ↓         ↑/
  fill     begin       backward  leaves     center
           wiggling              form       cut
```

**Detailed technique:**
1. **High pour:** Fill cup to ~35–40%; standard high pour
2. **Lower to surface:** Spout close to surface; begin pouring steadily
3. **Start wiggling:** Move pitcher rapidly left-right (small, consistent oscillations ~2–3cm each direction)
4. **Move backward:** While wiggling, slowly move pitcher toward the far side of the cup (away from you). Each wiggle leaves a "leaf" of the fern.
5. **Control speed:** Faster wiggling = more leaves; move backward at consistent pace
6. **Cut through:** When near far edge of cup, raise pitcher; pour thin stream through center of fern pattern from far edge back toward you
7. **Lift:** Complete the stem of the rosetta

**Key insights:**
- Backward movement speed determines leaf density
- Wiggle amplitude determines leaf width
- Symmetry comes with muscle memory — requires hundreds of pours

## Pattern 3: Tulip 🌷

**Difficulty:** Beginner–Intermediate | **Time to learn:** 1–3 weeks

### Step-by-Step

```
3 pulses → 3 stacked circles:

Pulse 1:    Pulse 2:    Pulse 3:    Cut:
  ●           ●           ●          ↑
(small)     (medium)    (large)     stem
  
Result: Stacked semicircle layers
```

**Detailed technique:**
1. **High pour:** Fill ~30%
2. **Lower pitcher:** Close to surface
3. **Pulse 1:** Short, controlled pour → small white dot appears → stop flow
4. **Pulse 2:** Move slightly closer (overlap with first dot) → slightly more milk → stop
5. **Pulse 3:** Largest pulse → largest circle, pushing previous circles up
6. **Cut:** Raise pitcher; thin stream through center from back to front to create stem
7. **Variations:** 2-layer (simple), 3-layer (standard), 4–6 layer (advanced)

## Pattern 4: Swan 🦢

**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Time to learn:** 3–6 months

The swan combines multiple patterns:
- **Wing 1:** Rosetta technique (one side of cup)
- **Wing 2:** Mirror rosetta (other side)
- **Neck/head:** Careful free-pour

This is a **competition-level pattern** requiring mastery of the rosetta first.

---

## Pattern 5: Phoenix / Peacock

**Difficulty:** Expert | **Time to learn:** 6+ months

Combines:
- Multiple tulip stacks
- Rosetta side wings
- Free-pour center

Only seen at World Latte Art Championship level — requires exceptional microfoam consistency and pour control.

## Competition Latte Art Standards (WLAC)

| Criterion | What Judges Look For |
|---------|---------------------|
| **Visual complexity** | More elements, more layers = higher score |
| **Symmetry** | Pattern must be balanced left-right |
| **Definition** | Sharp, clear contrast between milk and crema |
| **Creativity** | Novel patterns or creative reinterpretation |
| **Overall impression** | Is it beautiful? |
| **Consistency** | Both cups (duplication round) must match |

**Time limit:** Typically 8–10 minutes for 2 drinks

| Stage | Practice Focus | Milestone |
|-------|---------------|-----------|
| Week 1 | Steam consistent microfoam | Glossy, no visible bubbles every time |
| Week 2–3 | Heart pattern | Clear heart shape 7/10 pours |

## Troubleshooting Latte Art

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| No white appears | Foam too coarse / milk too hot | Better steaming; cooler milk |
| Pattern sinks immediately | Microfoam too thin | More rolling in steam phase |
| Lines blurry | Microfoam too thick/stiff | Less stretch; more rolling |
| Pattern off-center | Uneven cup angle or pour start | Tilt cup more; center initial pour |
| Rosetta asymmetric | Uneven wiggle oscillation | Practice consistency of wiggle |
| Heart has no point | Cut too slow or pitcher too high | Lower pitcher for cut; move faster |
| Crema destroyed | Espresso too old; shot dissipated | Pour within 30 seconds of pulling shot |
