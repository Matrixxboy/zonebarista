## Key Bioactive Compounds in Coffee

| Compound | Content (per 240mL cup) | Primary Health Role |
|---------|------------------------|---------------------|
| **Caffeine** | 80–200mg (varies widely) | CNS stimulant; adenosine antagonist |
| **Chlorogenic acids (CGAs)** | 200–550mg | Antioxidant; glucose metabolism; inflammation |
| **Trigonelline** | 50–120mg | Neuroprotective; glucose metabolism |
| **Cafestol + Kahweol** | 0–7mg (filter removes most) | Raise LDL cholesterol; anti-cancer research |
| **Melanoidins** | 200–400mg | Antioxidant; prebiotic activity |
| **Potassium** | ~120mg | Electrolyte; cardiovascular |
| **Magnesium** | ~7mg | Cofactor for 300+ enzymes |
| **Niacin (B3)** | ~1mg | Metabolised from trigonelline during roasting |

## Caffeine Physiology

### Mechanism of Action

Caffeine's primary mechanism is **competitive inhibition of adenosine receptors** (A₁ and A₂A):

```
Normal state:
Adenosine → binds A1/A2A receptors → DROWSY / REDUCED AROUSAL

With caffeine:
Caffeine → blocks adenosine receptors → ADENOSINE CANNOT BIND → ALERT
```

**Secondary effects:**
- Increases norepinephrine and dopamine release
- Stimulates adrenaline (epinephrine) production
- Inhibits phosphodiesterase → increases cAMP → heart rate ↑

### Caffeine Content by Brew Method

| Method | Caffeine (mg per 240mL) | Notes |
|--------|------------------------|-------|
| Filter/drip coffee | 95–200mg | Highly variable by dose and grind |
| Espresso (single) | 60–75mg | Less volume but concentrated |
| Espresso (double) | 120–150mg | Standard café double |
| Cold brew | 150–250mg | Long extraction; concentrated |
| French Press | 80–130mg | Full immersion |
| Instant coffee | 30–90mg | Lower extraction |
| Decaf | 2–15mg | Not zero — small amount remains |

> 🔬 *Myth: Espresso has the most caffeine.* Espresso has more caffeine per mL (TDS ~10%), but a typical 30mL shot has less caffeine than a 240mL filter coffee. Total caffeine depends on dose and brew volume.

### Caffeine Metabolism

Caffeine is metabolised primarily by **CYP1A2 enzyme** in the liver:

```
Caffeine (half-life: 3–7 hours)
    ↓ CYP1A2
Paraxanthine (main metabolite) → lipolysis stimulation
Theophylline → bronchodilation
Theobromine → mild stimulant, vasodilator
```

**Genetic variation:** CYP1A2 activity varies significantly between individuals:
- **Fast metabolisers:** Caffeine cleared quickly; less sensitivity; may require more for effect
- **Slow metabolisers:** Caffeine persists longer; greater cardiovascular sensitivity; higher sleep disruption risk

---

### Safe Consumption Guidelines

**EFSA (European Food Safety Authority) 2015 assessment:**

| Population | Safe Daily Dose |
|-----------|----------------|
| Healthy adults | Up to **400mg/day** |
| Pregnant women | Up to **200mg/day** |
| Adolescents (12–18) | Up to **3mg/kg body weight/day** |
| Children (<12) | Not recommended |

**Equivalent cups (approximate, filter coffee at 100mg/cup):**
- 400mg = ~4 cups/day maximum
- 200mg = ~2 cups/day (pregnancy)

---

### Caffeine & Sleep

Caffeine has a **half-life of 3–7 hours** (individual variation):

| Consumed at | 50% remains in system until |
|------------|----------------------------|
| 8:00 AM | 11:00 AM–3:00 PM |
| 12:00 PM | 3:00 PM–7:00 PM |
| 2:00 PM | 5:00 PM–9:00 PM |
| 4:00 PM | 7:00 PM–11:00 PM |
| 6:00 PM | 9:00 PM–1:00 AM |

**Practical guidance:** For most adults, no caffeine after 1–2 PM supports optimal sleep. For slow metabolisers, cut-off may need to be earlier.

## Health Effects — Research Summary

### Broadly Positive Associations (well-supported)

| Health Area | Evidence | Notes |
|------------|---------|-------|
| **Type 2 diabetes** | Strong inverse (↑ coffee → ↓ risk) | Multiple large meta-analyses; CGAs improve insulin sensitivity |
| **Liver disease** | Strong protective | Cirrhosis, NAFLD, liver cancer risk reduced |
| **Parkinson's disease** | Moderate inverse | Caffeine's adenosine antagonism may be protective |
| **Cognitive function (acute)** | Strong positive | Caffeine improves alertness, attention, reaction time |
| **Alzheimer's risk** | Emerging protective | Observational; CGAs and caffeine both implicated |
| **Depression risk** | Moderate inverse | Observational; multiple studies |
| **Cardiovascular (moderate intake)** | Neutral to mildly protective | U-shaped curve; 3–5 cups/day associated with lowest risk |
| **Longevity** | Moderate positive association | Poole 2017 BMJ umbrella review |

### Mixed or Neutral Evidence

| Health Area | Evidence | Notes |
|------------|---------|-------|
| **Bone density** | Weakly negative at high doses | High caffeine may slightly reduce calcium absorption; offset by milk addition |
| **Blood pressure (short-term)** | Mildly elevated acutely | Habitual consumption: tolerance develops; long-term effect minimal in most |
| **Atrial fibrillation** | Complex | Once thought harmful; recent evidence suggests no increased risk at moderate intake |
| **Anxiety** | Worsens at high doses | Individual sensitivity varies enormously; genetic CYP1A2 factor |

### Caution Areas

| Situation | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Pregnancy** | Limit to <200mg/day; consult healthcare provider |
| **Anxiety disorders** | May worsen symptoms; individual monitoring needed |
| **Acid reflux/GERD** | Coffee is acidic and increases stomach acid; aggravates symptoms |
| **Unfiltered coffee + cholesterol** | Cafestol/kahweol in French Press and espresso raise LDL; use paper filter if LDL is elevated |
| **Insomnia** | Timing and dose adjustment essential |
| **Children** | Not recommended for young children |

## Antioxidants in Coffee

Coffee is one of the **largest sources of antioxidants** in the Western diet — not because it has the highest concentration per gram, but because people consume large quantities:

### Chlorogenic Acids (CGAs) — Primary Antioxidants

**Mechanism:**
```
Free radical (ROS) → Damages DNA/proteins/lipids
CGA → Donates electron to neutralise free radical → Oxidised CGA (inactive)
```

| CGA benefit area | Research status |
|-----------------|----------------|
| Glucose metabolism / glycaemic control | Strong evidence |
| Inflammation reduction | Moderate evidence |
| LDL oxidation prevention | Moderate evidence |
| Neuroprotection | Emerging |

**Important note:** Roasting degrades CGAs. Light roast retains more CGAs than dark roast. However, Maillard products (melanoidins) in darker roasts also have antioxidant activity.

---

### Melanoidins — Roast-Derived Antioxidants

Melanoidins (brown polymers from Maillard reaction) have:
- **Antioxidant activity** — comparable to some pure antioxidants
- **Prebiotic activity** — may benefit gut microbiome
- **Anti-inflammatory properties** (in vitro evidence)
- **Antimicrobial activity** against some pathogens

## Filtered vs Unfiltered Coffee — Health Comparison

| Factor | Paper Filter | French Press / Espresso |
|--------|-------------|------------------------|
| Cafestol + Kahweol (LDL-raising) | Removed (~97%) | Present (raises LDL) |
| Coffee oils | Most removed | Present (body, mouthfeel) |
| Antioxidants (CGAs) | Pass through | Pass through |
| Recommendation for high LDL | Paper filter preferred | Limit or switch to filtered |

## Decaffeinated Coffee

### Decaffeination Methods

| Method | Solvent | Caffeine Removal | Flavour Impact |
|--------|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Swiss Water Process** | Water only (no chemicals) | >99.9% | Mild flavour loss |
| **CO₂ Process** | Supercritical CO₂ | >99% | Minimal flavour impact (best) |
| **Methylene Chloride (DCM)** | Solvent | 99%+ | Some residue concern (very low at roast) |
| **Ethyl Acetate (EA)** | Solvent (natural source) | 97%+ | Slight sweetness change |

**Residual caffeine:** Decaf contains 2–15mg per cup — not zero. Relevant for extreme caffeine sensitivity or late-night consumption.

**Health note:** Decaf retains most antioxidant benefits (CGAs, melanoidins) — caffeine removal doesn't remove these compounds.

---

## Specialty Coffee & Health

Higher-quality specialty coffee may offer additional health considerations:
- **Higher CGA content** in lighter roasts (less roasting = less CGA degradation)
- **Better water quality** used in specialty preparation
- **Lower dose per cup** (precision brewing = less over-extraction; less caffeine per volume)
- **Fresher coffee** = less oxidised compounds

However, these differences are modest — the fundamental health profile is similar across specialty vs commercial coffee at equivalent doses.
